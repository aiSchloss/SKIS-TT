English Literature Test - Answer Key

Work: "School Sufferings" by Daniel Pennac

1. The author's twofold experience consists of his time as a struggling, underachieving student ("dunce") and his later observations as an "advanced" and insightful teacher. This duality allows him to analyze the experience of academic failure from both inside and out, with empathy for the student and a critical eye on the educational system.

2. The "Djibouti Dump" represents the author's feeling of being a worthless outcast, a piece of trash discarded by the school system. It's a metaphor for his profound sense of failure and the rock-bottom of his self-esteem during his childhood, a place of intellectual and emotional desolation.

3. The author's mother is consumed by a deep and persistent anxiety about his future. It manifests as a constant, worried gaze and concerned questions (e.g., "Do you have an apartment in Paris?"), even long after he has become a successful writer and teacher. Her worry is a "fossilized anxiety" that his later success can never completely erase.

4. The first teacher to save him was his French teacher in the third grade. He recognized the author's imaginative talent, which was hidden behind his academic failures. The teacher freed him from the obligation of writing standard school essays and instead commissioned him to write a novel, one chapter per week, with the only condition being that it must be free of spelling errors. This gave the author a sense of status and purpose as a writer.

5. The self-perception of being a "dunce" paralyzed him intellectually. He believed he was incapable of understanding, which became a self-fulfilling prophecy. It created a barrier between him and the subjects being taught, making him feel that school was a "closed club" not meant for him. This led to him fabricating elaborate excuses and feeling a constant sense of shame and alienation.

6. The three other teachers who saved him were his mathematics, history, and philosophy teachers. Their common quality was a passionate, all-consuming love for their respective subjects ("they were possessed by their subject"). They taught with a creative and engaging style that made the subject matter itself a compelling event, which in turn captured the author's interest and helped him overcome his learning blocks.

7. His first significant love was with a student from a prestigious pedagogical school. For the first time, a woman he loved saw him not as a failure but as an intelligent and capable person. This belief in him, and his love for her, broke down his long-held sense of shame and intellectual paralysis. It unleashed his hidden knowledge and creativity, allowing him to succeed academically and as a writer.

8. The author's relationship with reading was a secret, personal escape. He read voraciously and for pleasure, hiding novels inside his textbooks. This "underground" reading was a source of comfort and a way to build his own inner world, separate from the judgment and failure of the classroom. It was the true source of his education, even if it didn't immediately improve his grades or spelling.

9. In the anecdote with the dog, the author learns that even a simple creature can demonstrate a form of logical reasoning that he, the "dunce," felt he lacked. The dog, having been kicked off the bed, brings its own bedding to be allowed back, connecting the abstract idea of "cleanliness" with the act of being permitted in the masters' space. This makes the author feel that even the dog is smarter than he is, deepening his sense of intellectual inadequacy.

10. The internal dialogue reveals that the scars of his school sufferings are permanent. The inner "dunce" voice still haunts him with self-doubt, criticism, and the fear of failure, even as a successful adult. It shows that the feeling of being an intellectual impostor and the shame associated with his early academic struggles have never fully disappeared, becoming a permanent part of his psyche.
